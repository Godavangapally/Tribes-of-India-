JHARKHAND





History


Jharkhand is a state in eastern India. The state shares its border with the states of West Bengal to the east, Chhattisgarh to the west, Uttar Pradesh to the northwest, Bihar to the north and Odisha to the south. It is the 15th largest state by area, and the 14th largest by population. Hindi is the official language of the state. The city of Ranchi is its capital and Dumka its sub-capital. The state is known for its waterfalls, hills and holy places; Baidyanath Dham, Parasnath, Dewri and Rajrappa are major religious sites.



List of Popular tribes in JHARKHAND


  • Santhal Tribe


    The Santhal tribe is one of the largest indigenous communities in Jharkhand, India, with a rich cultural heritage. They primarily inhabit the Chota Nagpur Plateau region and are also found in parts of West Bengal, Bihar, and Odisha. The Santhals have their own language, Santhali, which belongs to the Austroasiatic language family. Traditionally, they are known for their agricultural practices, particularly rice cultivation, as well as hunting and gathering. Santhal society is characterized by a strong sense of community, with traditional customs, rituals, and festivals playing a significant role in their social fabric.


  • MUNDA


    Munda means headman of a village in the Munda-Manki system to govern villages in South-east Chotanagpur. They call themselves horoko or ho ko, which means men.Robert Parkin notes that the term "Munda" did not belong to the Austroasiatic lexis and is of Sanskrit origin.According to R. R. Prasad, the name "Munde" is a Ho word that means "headman". It is an honorific name given by Hindus, and hence became a tribal name. According to Standing (1976), it was under British rule that the term Munda started to be used for the tribal group.


  • ORAON


    The Oraon tribe is one of the prominent indigenous communities in Jharkhand, India, with a significant presence in other states like Bihar, Odisha, and West Bengal. They primarily inhabit the Chota Nagpur Plateau region. The Oraons traditionally practice agriculture, with rice cultivation being a central component of their livelihood. They have their own distinct language, Kurukh, which belongs to the Dravidian language family. Oraon society is characterized by a rich oral tradition, cultural rituals, dances, and festivals that play a vital role in their social cohesion and identity.
