SIKKIM





History


Sikkim is a state in northeastern India. It borders the Tibet Autonomous Region of China in the north and northeast, Bhutan in the east, Koshi Province of Nepal in the west, and West Bengal in the south. Sikkim is also close to the Siliguri Corridor, which borders Bangladesh. Sikkim is the least populous and second smallest among the Indian states. Situated in the Eastern Himalaya, Sikkim is notable for its biodiversity, including alpine and subtropical climates, as well as being a host to Kangchenjunga, the highest peak in India and third highest on Earth. Sikkim's capital and largest city is Gangtok.



List of Popular tribes in SIKKIM


  • Bhutia Tribe


    The Bhutia tribe is an ethnic group primarily residing in the Himalayan regions of India, Nepal, and Bhutan. They trace their origins to Tibet and have a distinct culture closely tied to Tibetan traditions. Traditional Bhutia attire includes the Bakhu for men and the Honju for women, reflecting their rich cultural heritage. Their traditional religion is Tibetan Buddhism, with monasteries and Buddhist rituals playing a central role in their community life. Despite modernization, efforts are being made to preserve Bhutia culture and address socio-economic challenges faced by the community.

  • Lepcha Tribe


    The Lepcha tribe is an indigenous community primarily found in the Himalayan regions of Sikkim, Darjeeling, and parts of Nepal and Bhutan. They have their own distinct language, known as Lepcha, which belongs to the Tibeto-Burman language family. Lepcha culture is rich in traditions, with a strong emphasis on nature worship and animistic beliefs. Traditional Lepcha attire includes colorful woven garments adorned with intricate designs. The Lepcha people traditionally practiced shifting agriculture and are known for their expertise in herbal medicine and handicrafts.


  • sherpa


    The Sherpa tribe is an ethnic group native to the Himalayan region, primarily concentrated in Nepal's Solukhumbu district and parts of Tibet. Renowned for their mountaineering prowess, Sherpas have played crucial roles as guides and porters on expeditions to Mount Everest and other peaks. They speak Sherpa, a Tibetic language, and practice Tibetan Buddhism, with monasteries such as Tengboche Monastery being significant cultural centers. Traditional Sherpa attire includes the "chuba," a long robe, and distinctive headgear. Despite modernization and the tourism industry's impact, Sherpas maintain strong cultural traditions and a close-knit community.
